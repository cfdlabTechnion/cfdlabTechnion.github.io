Abstract

Deep neural networks are known to exhibit a spectral learning bias, wherein low-frequency components are learned early in the training, while high-frequency modes emerge more gradually in the later epochs. However, when the target signal lacks low-frequency components and is dominated by broadband high frequencies, training suffers from a spectral bottleneck. As a result, the model fails to reconstruct the entire signal, including the frequency components that lie within its representational capacity. We examine such a scenario in the context of implicit neural representation networks (INRs), focusing on the challenge of fitting high-frequency-dominant signals that are susceptible to spectral bottleneck. To address this, we propose a generalized target-aware weight perturbation scheme for network initialization. The scheme perturbs uniformly initialized network weights with broadband Gaussian noise, broadening the spectral profile of activations at initialization. The proposed approach not only addresses the spectral bottleneck, but also improves the accuracy of INRs, outperforming state-of-the-art methods in audio fitting and achieving notable gains in image fitting and denoising. Beyond signal reconstruction, our approach opens new directions for adaptive initialization strategies in neural representation tasks, particularly those requiring fine-scale detail.
